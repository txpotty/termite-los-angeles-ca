Identifying & Mitigating Risk Factors

Understanding the factors that attract termites is crucial for prevention. Termites are drawn to moisture and wood. Common Termite Problems & When to Act

Recognizing the signs of a termite infestation is crucial for prompt action. Termites can cause significant damage before you even realize they are there. Knowing what to look for can help you catch problems early and protect your home. If you notice any of these signs, it is highly recommended to contact a professional for an inspection and potential treatment. Acting quickly can save you considerable expense and stress in the long run.

Mud Tubes on Walls or Foundation

Subterranean termites, the most common type in Los Angeles, build distinctive mud tubes (often called ‘shelter tubes’) on exterior or interior walls, foundations, or other structures. These tubes, typically the width of a pencil, serve as protective pathways for termites as they travel between their underground colony and their food source (your home’s wood). The mud helps maintain moisture and protects them from predators and dry environments. Discovering these tubes is a strong indicator of a subterranean termite infestation and requires immediate professional attention. Disturbing these tubes is not recommended as it can cause the termites to simply relocate and continue their destructive activity elsewhere in your home, potentially making detection and treatment more challenging. It’s best to leave them intact and call a professional who can properly assess the situation and determine the extent of the infestation connected to those tubes.

Discarded Termite Wings

Termite swarmers, or reproductive termites, are often the first visible sign of an infestation. These winged termites emerge from the colony during certain times of the year to build new colonies. After swarming, they shed their wings. Finding small piles of discarded wings, particularly near windowsills, doorways, or other light sources where they may have been attracted, is a tell-tale sign of a nearby termite colony. The size and shape of the wings can also help identify the species of termite involved, which is important for determining the most effective treatment method. Don’t confuse termite wings with ant wings; termite wings are typically all the same size and shape, while ant wings are usually different sizes. Seeing discarded wings indicates that a mature termite colony is active on or near your property and that the infestation has reached a level where reproductive termites are being produced. This is a clear signal that professional intervention is necessary to prevent further damage and establish a new, potentially larger, colony.

Wood Damage

Termites primarily feed on cellulose, which is found in wood. Termite damage can appear in various forms depending on the type of termite and the extent of the infestation. Subterranean termites often eat the wood from the inside out, leaving a thin veneer of wood or paint. This can make the wood sound hollow when tapped. Drywood termites, on the other hand, create tunnels and galleries within the wood, and their presence can sometimes be indicated by small, pellet-like droppings called frass. Examining exposed wood in crawl spaces, basements, attics, and even within your home’s walls can reveal signs of termite activity. Look for darkened or blistering wood, buckling paint, or small holes. Wood damage may not be immediately obvious, and a professional inspection is often required to uncover hidden infestations. The type and pattern of wood damage can provide valuable clues about the termite species and the severity of the problem, guiding the most effective treatment strategy.

Swarming Termites

Seeing actual winged termites swarming, especially indoors, is a definitive indication of a termite infestation. Swarming often occurs when a colony has grown large enough to produce reproductive termites, who leave the nest to establish new colonies. Swarming typically happens during specific times of the year, often after rain and during warm weather. While seeing swarmers can be alarming, it also presents an opportunity to locate the source of the infestation, as the swarmers usually emerge from the nest. If you observe swarming termites inside your home, it strongly suggests that a termite colony is already established within the structure. Swarming termites outside may indicate a colony on your property or a neighboring one. In either case, it warrants an expert inspection to identify the source and extent of the infestation and implement appropriate control measures. Documenting the time of year and appearance of the swarmers can also be helpful information for the pest control professional.

Termite Droppings (Frass)

Drywood termites produce small, hard, pellet-like fecal droppings called frass. These droppings are pushed out of small ‘kick-out’ holes they create in the infested wood. Finding small piles of these pellets, which resemble coarse sawdust or tiny grains of sand, is a key indicator of a drywood termite infestation. The color of the frass can vary depending on the type of wood the termites are consuming. These pellets are usually found near or below areas of drywood termite activity. Unlike subterranean termites which leave mud tubes, drywood termites leave behind these distinct droppings as the primary visible sign of their presence. If you discover what appears to be sawdust or wood pellets in areas like windowsills, doorframes, or furniture, it’s likely a sign of drywood termites and you should contact a professional for verification and treatment. Ignoring these small piles could lead to significant damage to the infested wooden structures over time as the colony continues to consume the wood from within.

Hollow-Sounding Wood

Tapping on wood surfaces suspected of being infested with termites can reveal whether it has been compromised. Wood that has been eaten from the inside by subterranean termites will often sound hollow when tapped. This is because the termites have consumed the interior cellulose, leaving only a thin shell of wood or paint. This can be a subtle sign of infestation and may require a keen ear to detect. Examining areas around potential entry points, such as window frames, door frames, and baseboards, is a good starting point for this type of inspection. You can use a screwdriver handle or other blunt object to lightly tap on the wood surface and listen for changes in sound. A solid, resonant sound indicates healthy wood, while a dull, hollow sound may suggest termite damage. This technique is especially helpful when visual signs of damage are not immediately apparent but you have other reasons to suspect termite activity, such as observing mud tubes or swarmers in the past. However, this method is not foolproof and a professional inspection provides a more definitive assessment of the structural integrity of the wood and the presence of termites.

Bubbling or Peeling Paint

While not always a direct sign of termites, bubbling or peeling paint can sometimes indicate moisture issues that attract termites or signal underlying wood damage caused by termites. If the paint is bubbling or peeling in areas where there is no apparent water leak, it could be a sign that termites are present beneath the surface, creating moisture and damage that are affecting the paint. As termites consume wood, they can create conditions that lead to blistering or bubbling of the paint finish. This is particularly true with subterranean termites and their need for moisture. The damage caused by termites can also weaken the wood beneath the paint, leading to its deterioration and the paint lifting away from the surface. If you notice unusual bubbling or peeling paint, especially in conjunction with other potential termite signs, it is worth investigating further with a professional inspection to rule out or confirm a termite infestation. Don’t dismiss this symptom simply as a paint defect; it could be an indicator of a deeper structural issue caused by termite activity that requires prompt attention.

Stuck Doors or Windows

Doors and windows that become difficult to open or close smoothly, or appear warped, could also be a sign of termite infestation, particularly if the frames are made of wood. Termite activity and the associated moisture they bring can cause wooden frames to warp, swell, or buckle, making is challenging for doors and windows to operate correctly. As termites consume the wood, the structural integrity of the frame can be compromised, leading to misalignment and difficulty in movement. This symptom is more likely to be associated with significant termite damage rather than an early infestation. If you’re experiencing suddenly difficult to operate doors or windows and can’t identify another cause, such as humidity or foundation issues, it’s wise to consider the possibility of a termite infestation. A professional can inspect the frames and surrounding areas to determine if termites are the culprit behind the operational problems. Addressing the termite issue will be necessary to resolve the problem with the doors or windows and prevent further damage to the structural components of your home.
